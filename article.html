Residents of Vancouver, BC are struggling to cope with the aftermath of a massive winter storm that swept through the city, causing widespread damage and disruptions to daily life.

The storm, which began on Tuesday evening and lasted for over 24 hours, brought heavy snowfall, high winds, and icy conditions to the region. Many roads and highways were closed, and public transportation was severely impacted, with buses and trains delayed or canceled.

That has prompted the City of Vancouver to implement its cold weather response through to Monday. That means there will be additional shelter spaces and warming centres available for unhoused people.

Thousands of homes and businesses lost power as trees and power lines were knocked down by the strong winds. Emergency responders were inundated with calls for help, with many residents reporting injuries due to falls on icy sidewalks or car accidents on slippery roads.

The city's mayor, John Smith, declared a state of emergency on Wednesday morning and urged residents to stay home and avoid unnecessary travel. "This is an extremely dangerous situation, and we are asking everyone to take it seriously," he said in a press conference. "Our first priority is the safety and well-being of our residents, and we are working around the clock to ensure that emergency services are available to those in need."

The storm also had a significant impact on the local economy, with many businesses forced to close and events canceled or rescheduled. The Vancouver International Airport was also affected, with many flights delayed or canceled.

As the storm subsided on Thursday morning, residents were left to deal with the aftermath of the extreme weather event. Many began shoveling snow and clearing debris from their homes and streets, while others sought out emergency shelters and warming centers.

The mayor announced that cleanup and recovery efforts would be underway for the foreseeable future, and he thanked emergency responders and city workers for their efforts in responding to the storm. "We know that this has been a difficult and trying time for everyone in our city," he said. "But we will get through this together, and we will emerge stronger and more resilient as a community."
